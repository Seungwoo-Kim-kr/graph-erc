\begin{abstract}
Emotion recognition in conversation (ERC) requires models to ground their
predictions in conversational evidence rather than relying solely on
parametric knowledge. This paper presents a systematic evaluation of
six context-construction strategies for LLM-based ERC: LLM-only,
full-context, BM25 retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), dense RAG,
text-only RAG, and graph-augmented RAG. Beyond standard classification
metrics, a claim-level faithfulness evaluation is introduced that measures
whether model-generated explanations are supported by the retrieved
evidence. Experiments on MELD and EmoryNLP reveal consistent findings
across both benchmarks. On MELD, LLM-only inference achieves the
highest accuracy (0.618) but near-zero faithfulness (0.014), suggesting
that generated explanations are rarely grounded in the supplied evidence;
graph-augmented RAG achieves the best macro F1 among retrieval methods
(0.491) while maintaining competitive faithfulness (0.222). On EmoryNLP,
graph-augmented RAG again leads all methods in macro F1 (0.471) and
accuracy (0.500), and the faithfulness ordering is consistent with MELD.
Ablation analysis on MELD identifies same-speaker history as the most
critical graph component ($-$0.053 macro F1 on removal). Together,
these findings indicate that retrieval-augmented context construction
substantially improves both predictive quality and evidence faithfulness
over no-retrieval baselines, and that faithfulness serves as a useful
complement to accuracy for evaluating LLM-based ERC systems.

\keywords{Emotion Recognition in Conversation \and
Retrieval-Augmented Generation \and Graph-Augmented Retrieval \and
Faithfulness Evaluation \and Large Language Models}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Emotion recognition in conversation (ERC) is a central task in social
and behavioural computing, enabling applications in dialogue analysis,
mental health support, human-robot interaction, and education
technology~\cite{poria2019meld,zahiri2018emorynlp}. Unlike
sentence-level emotion classification, ERC requires the model to
integrate contextual signals spanning multiple utterances, speaker
relationships, and discourse dynamics.

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities
in natural language understanding, and recent studies have begun
applying them to ERC with strong results~\cite{zhao2023chatgpt,wang2023instructerc}.
However, a fundamental question remains: when an LLM predicts an
emotion, is the prediction grounded in the conversational evidence
actually provided, or does it reflect the model's internal parametric
knowledge about how dialogue typically unfolds? In socially sensitive
settings, the ability to trace a prediction back to specific
conversational evidence is as important as classification accuracy
itself.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)~\cite{lewis2020rag} offers a
natural mechanism for grounding LLM outputs by explicitly supplying
relevant context at inference time. For ERC, retrieval can select the
most emotionally relevant prior utterances, reducing noise from
irrelevant context. Graph-augmented retrieval extends this idea by
representing dialogue as a structured graph of speaker and emotional
relationships, enabling more principled evidence selection that reflects
the social dynamics of conversation.

Despite growing interest in RAG for dialogue, prior work has
predominantly focused on accuracy metrics, leaving the faithfulness of
model reasoning under-explored. This paper addresses this gap by
jointly evaluating predictive performance and evidence faithfulness
across six context-construction strategies. Three research questions
guide the study: (RQ1) Does retrieval-augmented context construction
improve the faithfulness of LLM-based ERC compared with no-retrieval
and full-context baselines? (RQ2) Does graph-structured evidence
improve prediction quality over flat retrieval while maintaining
competitive faithfulness? (RQ3) Which graph components contribute most
to the improvement, and are their contributions consistent across
prediction and faithfulness metrics?

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Systematic context-construction benchmark.}
    A comparative evaluation of six LLM-based ERC strategies is presented,
    spanning no-retrieval, flat retrieval, and graph-augmented retrieval
    across the MELD and EmoryNLP benchmarks under a unified experimental protocol.

    \item \textbf{Claim-level faithfulness evaluation framework.}
    A novel two-step faithfulness evaluation pipeline is introduced in which
    model explanations are decomposed into atomic claims and each claim is
    independently verified against the retrieved evidence by an LLM-as-judge,
    yielding a continuous faithfulness score alongside standard accuracy metrics.

    \item \textbf{Evidence of a systematic faithfulness gap.}
    Experiments reveal that LLM-only and full-context methods achieve
    near-zero faithfulness (below 0.04), whereas all retrieval-augmented
    methods raise faithfulness by an order of magnitude (above 0.21),
    independent of classification accuracy.

    \item \textbf{Graph component ablation.}
    An ablation study identifies same-speaker history as the dominant
    structural signal in graph-augmented retrieval, whose removal causes
    a 0.053 drop in macro F1—larger than any other graph component.

    \item \textbf{Class-imbalance failure mode.}
    Analysis on EmoryNLP reveals that zero-shot prompting collapses under
    severe class imbalance regardless of retrieval strategy, establishing
    class-balanced evaluation as a prerequisite for meaningful comparison
    on skewed dialogue datasets.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Emotion Recognition in Conversation}

Emotion recognition in conversation has been studied through a range of
neural architectures that progressively incorporate richer contextual
signals. Early recurrent approaches, such as the Conversational Memory
Network~\cite{hazarika2018conversational}, used memory-augmented networks
to track individual speaker states across dialogue turns.
DialogueRNN~\cite{majumder2019dialoguernn} extended this with attentive
gating that models the emotional influence of one speaker on another.
Graph-based methods then introduced relational inductive biases:
DialogueGCN~\cite{ghosal2019dialoguegcn} encodes speaker-level and
temporal dependencies through graph convolution, while
COSMIC~\cite{ghosal2020cosmic} integrates commonsense knowledge graphs
to supply implicit causal and emotional background knowledge.
DAG-ERC~\cite{shen2021dag} further addresses long-range context through
a directed acyclic graph that combines the strengths of recurrent and
graph-based representations. More recent work, such as
SKIER~\cite{li2023skier}, jointly models discourse-structure graphs and
commonsense knowledge subgraphs, establishing strong supervised baselines
on benchmarks including MELD and IEMOCAP.

Despite these advances, supervised ERC models are tightly coupled to
their training distributions and require large amounts of emotion-annotated
dialogue data. They also do not produce natural-language explanations,
making it difficult to audit whether a prediction is grounded in specific
conversational evidence. Furthermore, minority emotion classes remain
severely underperforming in all existing systems, indicating that the
problem is not yet solved by architectural innovation alone. This paper
departs from the supervised paradigm entirely and instead evaluates
whether an LLM prompted with structured graph evidence can reason
faithfully about emotion from first principles.

\subsection{LLMs for Social Dialogue Understanding}

The advent of instruction-tuned large language models has opened a new
direction for ERC that bypasses task-specific fine-tuning.
Wei et al.~\cite{wei2022flan} demonstrated that instruction fine-tuning
generalises across diverse NLP tasks in a zero-shot manner, providing the
methodological foundation for prompting-based ERC.
Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2023chatgpt} then evaluated ChatGPT on sentiment
reasoning benchmarks and showed competitive zero-shot performance relative
to fine-tuned models. Tu et al.~\cite{tu2023chatgpt} further investigated
how multiple forms of knowledge elicited from ChatGPT, including
commonsense, causal, and contextual signals, can serve as auxiliary
supervision for ERC. InstructERC~\cite{wang2023instructerc} reformulates
ERC as a generative task with a retrieval-augmented template module and
trains a LLaMA backbone with emotion-alignment objectives, matching
or exceeding supervised baselines on several benchmarks. Liu et
al.~\cite{liu2024emollms} extend this line of work with a family of
open-source instruction-following LLMs specifically fine-tuned for
affective analysis, outperforming GPT-4 on several sentiment and emotion
classification benchmarks.

A consistent limitation across all these studies is their exclusive focus
on classification accuracy. None of them examine whether the model's
predictions or generated explanations are actually grounded in the
conversational evidence provided in the prompt. Consequently, high
accuracy scores may reflect the model's parametric knowledge about typical
emotional expressions rather than genuine reasoning over the supplied
dialogue. This gap motivates the faithfulness-oriented evaluation
framework proposed in this paper, which treats grounding in retrieved evidence as a first-class
evaluation criterion alongside predictive accuracy.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}

Retrieval-augmented generation was introduced by Lewis et
al.~\cite{lewis2020rag} as a general framework for grounding LLM outputs
in externally retrieved evidence. At inference time, a retriever selects
relevant passages from a corpus, and these are concatenated to the input
before generation. Karpukhin et al.~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr} showed that
dense passage retrieval using bi-encoder architectures substantially
outperforms BM25~\cite{robertson2009bm25} on open-domain question
answering. Reimers and Gurevych~\cite{reimers2019sbert} further improved
semantic retrieval by training siamese BERT networks with contrastive
objectives, enabling efficient cosine-similarity search over large
sentence corpora. More recent systems, such as Self-RAG~\cite{asai2024selfrag},
train the generator to adaptively decide when retrieval is necessary and
to self-critique the relevance and factuality of retrieved passages using
special reflection tokens. Gao et al.~\cite{gao2023survey} provide a
comprehensive taxonomy of RAG architectures and highlight retrieval quality
as the primary bottleneck for downstream generation fidelity.

Despite this progress, RAG has been developed almost exclusively for
open-domain question answering and knowledge-intensive generation tasks.
Its application to dialogue emotion recognition raises new challenges:
the retrieval corpus is the conversation window itself rather than an
external knowledge base, the relevant evidence is often implicit and
relationally distributed across turns, and the gold standard for
relevance is emotional rather than factual. Existing RAG systems
also provide no mechanism for verifying whether the generator actually
uses the retrieved evidence in its reasoning. This paper adapts and
systematically compares multiple retrieval strategies in this
under-explored dialogue setting.

\subsection{Graph-Augmented Retrieval for Dialogue}

Graph-based representations have been widely adopted in ERC to capture
relational structure that flat sequential models miss. As discussed above,
DialogueGCN~\cite{ghosal2019dialoguegcn}, COSMIC~\cite{ghosal2020cosmic},
DAG-ERC~\cite{shen2021dag}, and SKIER~\cite{li2023skier} each construct
different types of speaker and relational graphs over dialogue turns,
consistently improving over recurrent baselines. Beyond ERC classification,
the Knowledge-Enriched Transformer~\cite{zhong2019ket} augments transformer
self-attention with a context-aware affective graph over ConceptNet,
dynamically injecting commonsense knowledge at each utterance representation
during dialogue understanding.

The above methods all operate in the supervised learning paradigm, where
graph structures are trained end-to-end together with an emotion
classifier. As a result, the graph serves as an implicit inductive bias
rather than an explicit, inspectable source of evidence. In contrast,
the proposed approach serialises the dialogue graph into natural language and
provides it directly to an LLM as structured evidence. This design allows
the contribution of each graph component to be isolated and measured
through ablation, and enables faithfulness evaluation by checking whether
the LLM's explanation cites the relational information from the graph.

\subsection{Faithfulness and Evidence-Grounded Evaluation}

Faithfulness evaluation in natural language generation was first
systematically studied in the context of abstractive summarisation.
Maynez et al.~\cite{maynez2020faithfulness} established that a large
fraction of abstractive summaries contain facts not entailed by the
source document, and proposed intrinsic versus extrinsic hallucination
as a taxonomy. The FEVER benchmark~\cite{thorne2018fever} operationalised
factual verification at scale by constructing 185,000 claims against
Wikipedia and requiring systems to retrieve evidence and classify each
claim as SUPPORTED, REFUTED, or NOT ENOUGH INFO. Min et
al.~\cite{min2023factscore} introduced FActScore, which decomposes
long-form LLM generations into atomic facts and verifies each against a
retrieval-backed knowledge source, achieving near-human agreement on
biographical generation. Liu et al.~\cite{liu2023geval} propose G-EVAL,
a GPT-4-based evaluation framework that uses chain-of-thought prompting
to assess NLG dimensions including consistency and faithfulness, achieving
substantially higher correlation with human judgments than prior
reference-based metrics. For RAG systems specifically, Es et
al.~\cite{es2023ragas} propose RAGAS, a reference-free suite that
measures faithfulness by checking whether generated statements are
logically entailed by the retrieved context.

Despite this progress, all existing faithfulness evaluation frameworks
target summarisation, open-domain question answering, or general-purpose
generation tasks. None have been adapted to conversational emotion
recognition, where the evidence consists of emotionally salient dialogue
turns rather than factual passages, and where the grounding claim is
about the emotional state of a speaker rather than a verifiable fact.
Moreover, no prior work on LLM-based ERC has examined whether
model explanations are grounded in the supplied context. The claim-level
faithfulness evaluation framework proposed here bridges this gap by adapting the
atomic-claim decomposition and verification paradigm to the conversational
emotion recognition setting.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $D = \{u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_T\}$ denote a dialogue, where each
utterance $u_t = (s_t, x_t)$ consists of speaker $s_t$ and text $x_t$.
Given a target utterance $u_\tau$ and its conversational context
$C_\tau = \{u_t : t < \tau\}$, the task is to predict the emotion
label $y_\tau \in \mathcal{Y}$ and produce a natural language
explanation $e_\tau$ grounded in the available evidence.

\subsection{Context-Construction Methods}

Six methods are compared that differ in how they construct the evidence
provided to the LLM:

\paragraph{LLM-only.}
Only the target utterance $u_\tau$ is included in the prompt. No
contextual evidence is supplied, so any prediction must rely on the
model's parametric knowledge.

\paragraph{Full-context LLM.}
The complete preceding context $C_\tau$ is appended to the prompt,
providing maximal information but potentially introducing noise from
utterances unrelated to the target emotion.

\paragraph{BM25 RAG.}
A BM25 index~\cite{robertson2009bm25} is built over $C_\tau$, and the
top-$k$ utterances most lexically similar to the target are retrieved
as evidence.

\paragraph{Dense RAG.}
Utterances in $C_\tau$ are encoded by a sentence transformer
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}. The top-$k$ utterances with the highest
cosine similarity to the target embedding are retrieved.

\paragraph{Text-only RAG.}
The three most recent utterances in $C_\tau$ are provided as plain
text without retrieval scoring or graph structure.
This baseline controls for the effect of structure versus content.

\paragraph{Graph-augmented RAG.}
A dialogue graph $G_\tau = (V, E)$ is constructed over $C_\tau$, where
each node $v \in V$ represents an utterance. Edges encode three types
of relationships: (i) \emph{same-speaker links} connecting utterances
by the same speaker to capture within-speaker emotional continuity;
(ii) \emph{emotion-shift links} connecting adjacent utterances where
a notable emotional transition is detected (specifically, when consecutive
utterances differ in predicted sentiment polarity according to a
rule-based lexicon classifier); and (iii) \emph{sequential
adjacency links} connecting temporally adjacent utterances.
Graph-based evidence is then serialised into natural language and
included in the prompt alongside the retrieved utterances.

\paragraph{Graph serialisation.}
Each selected utterance node is serialised as a labelled record
specifying its speaker, turn index, text, and relationship type to
the target (e.g.,
\texttt{[same-speaker, turn~3] Speaker~A: ``I hate this!''}).
Nodes are ranked by edge degree in $G_\tau$, and the top-$k$
highest-degree nodes are included to bound the prompt length while
prioritising the most relationally connected utterances. This
serialisation makes the graph's relational structure directly
inspectable by the LLM and by the faithfulness evaluator.

\subsection{Prompting Strategy}
\label{sec:prompting}

All methods share a consistent evidence-constrained prompt format
designed to encourage faithfulness. The system instruction states that
the model must base its explanation \emph{only} on the provided
evidence and must not introduce external knowledge. The user message
contains three blocks: \texttt{[Dialogue Context]}, \texttt{[Target
Utterance]}, and \texttt{[Retrieved Evidence]}. The model is instructed
to output a JSON object with three fields:
\texttt{predicted\_emotion}, \texttt{explanation}, and
\texttt{evidence\_used}. The evidence-constrained prompt includes an
explicit rule set:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The explanation \emph{must} directly quote or cite specific
    utterances from \texttt{[Retrieved Evidence]}.
    \item The model must not introduce background knowledge or
    assumptions not present in the evidence.
    \item If evidence is insufficient, this must be stated explicitly.
    \item The \texttt{evidence\_used} field must list the exact phrases
    relied upon.
\end{enumerate}

The three-block structure deliberately separates \texttt{[Dialogue
Context]} (the broader conversational history) from \texttt{[Retrieved
Evidence]} (the specifically selected utterances), making the grounding
requirement operational: any claim in the explanation must be traceable
to the retrieved evidence rather than to arbitrary content in the broader
context. This separation is what enables the claim-level faithfulness
evaluation to attribute specific model claims to specific evidence items.
For the LLM-only baseline, \texttt{[Retrieved Evidence]} is explicitly
marked as empty, ensuring the model is aware that no external evidence
has been provided.

\subsection{Claim-Level Faithfulness Evaluation}
\label{sec:faithfulness}

Standard accuracy metrics cannot measure whether a prediction is
supported by the provided evidence. A two-step
claim-level faithfulness evaluation is adopted:

\paragraph{Step 1 — Claim decomposition.}
Given a model explanation $e_\tau$, an LLM judge decomposes it into a
set of atomic, independently verifiable claims
$\{c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n\}$.

\paragraph{Step 2 — Claim verification.}
Each claim $c_i$ is presented to the judge together with the dialogue
context and retrieved evidence. The judge assigns one of three verdicts:
\emph{supported} (score 1.0), \emph{partially supported} (score 0.5),
or \emph{unsupported} (score 0.0).

The faithfulness score for a prediction is the mean score over all
extracted claims:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Faithfulness}(e_\tau) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{score}(c_i)
\end{equation}

Both decomposition and verification are performed by \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}
with temperature 0 for reproducibility. The supported ratio and
unsupported ratio are also reported as secondary faithfulness metrics.

The decomposition prompt instructs the judge to extract each distinct
factual or inferential claim from $e_\tau$ as a self-contained sentence.
The verification prompt presents the judge with a single claim $c_i$,
the retrieved evidence, and the dialogue context, and asks it to
determine whether $c_i$ is directly entailed, partially entailed, or
not entailed by the supplied material. To mitigate positivity bias, the
prompt explicitly instructs the judge to return \emph{unsupported} for
any claim that relies on general world knowledge not present in the
retrieved evidence, even if the claim appears plausible.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Datasets}

\paragraph{MELD.} The Multimodal EmotionLines Dataset~\cite{poria2019meld}
is derived from the TV series \textit{Friends} and contains 13{,}708
utterances across 1{,}433 multi-party dialogues. Each utterance is
annotated with one of seven emotion labels: \textit{anger, disgust,
fear, joy, neutral, sadness,} and \textit{surprise}. The test split
contains 2{,}610 utterances across 280 dialogues.

\paragraph{EmoryNLP.} The EmoryNLP dataset~\cite{zahiri2018emorynlp}
is also sourced from \textit{Friends} but uses a different annotation
scheme with eight labels: \textit{mad, joyful, peaceful, neutral, sad,
scared, powerful,} and \textit{surprised}. The test split contains
1{,}328 utterances across 85 scenes. Due to the severe class imbalance
in EmoryNLP (neutral and joyful together account for $>$70\% of
instances), macro F1 is the primary evaluation metric.

\subsection{Model and Inference}

All experiments use \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} as the inference model with
temperature 0 and a maximum of 512 output tokens, ensuring deterministic
outputs across runs. The same model is used as the faithfulness judge.
All experiments use 500 instances uniformly sampled at random (seed 42)
from the test split to control API cost while maintaining statistical
coverage.

\subsection{Retrieval Settings}

For BM25 and dense retrieval, the top $k=5$ utterances are retrieved
from the context window $C_\tau$. For dense retrieval,
\texttt{sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2} is used as the
sentence encoder. The context window for text-only RAG consists of
the three most recent utterances. For graph-augmented RAG, the graph
is built over the same context window, and evidence is serialised using
a natural-language template.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\paragraph{Classification metrics.} The evaluation reports accuracy, macro-averaged
F1 (macro F1), weighted F1, worst-class F1, and per-class F1 for each
method. Macro F1 is the primary metric because it treats all emotion
classes equally regardless of frequency. Worst-class F1 is defined as
the minimum per-class F1 score across all emotion classes, measuring
performance on the hardest-to-classify emotion.

\paragraph{Faithfulness metrics.} The evaluation also reports the mean faithfulness
score, supported ratio (fraction of fully supported claims), and
unsupported ratio (fraction of unsupported claims) averaged across
all evaluated instances. Due to occasional API response parsing failures,
the effective sample size for faithfulness evaluation may be lower than
500 for some methods; results are computed over successfully parsed
responses only.

\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{MELD Classification Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:overall-performance} reports classification performance
on the MELD test set. Graph RAG achieves the highest macro F1 (0.491) among
retrieval-augmented methods, outperforming Text-only RAG by 0.011 and
BM25 RAG by 0.038. Full-context LLM achieves a comparable macro F1
(0.491) by providing the complete dialogue history, but at the cost of
near-zero faithfulness (0.033), as shown in Section~\ref{sec:faith-results}.
When prediction quality and faithfulness are considered jointly, Graph
RAG is the only method that simultaneously achieves macro F1 above 0.49
and faithfulness above 0.22, making it the most balanced choice when
both classification accuracy and explainability are required.
LLM-only achieves the highest accuracy (0.618) overall, likely because
the Friends TV corpus underlying MELD is well-represented in the
model's pre-training data, enabling parametric recall without contextual
grounding. Text-only RAG records the best worst-class F1 (0.333) among
all methods, indicating that recency-based context selection helps
minority classes more than semantic retrieval.

\subsection{Faithfulness Analysis}
\label{sec:faith-results}

Table~\ref{tab:faithfulness} reports faithfulness metrics, providing
a direct answer to RQ1. The most striking finding is the near-total
absence of faithfulness in non-retrieval methods: LLM-only achieves a
mean faithfulness score of only 0.014, with 98.4\% of claims rated
unsupported. Full-context LLM improves marginally to 0.033, still with
96.3\% unsupported claims. This reveals that even when the full dialogue is provided, the
generated explanations are rarely grounded in the supplied context
under the proposed evaluation protocol. All retrieval
methods raise faithfulness by more than an order of magnitude (from
below 0.04 to above 0.21), confirming that explicit evidence retrieval
is necessary for grounded reasoning in ERC.

Among retrieval methods, BM25 RAG achieves the highest faithfulness
(0.269), followed by Dense RAG (0.256), Graph RAG (0.222), and
Text-only RAG (0.213). The ordering reflects the nature of each
retrieval strategy: BM25 selects utterances that share surface-level
vocabulary with the target, making them directly quotable in the
explanation. In contrast, graph-based evidence prioritises
relationally informative utterances (e.g., same-speaker history) that
are semantically relevant but may not share explicit wording with the
target, resulting in lower literal cite-ability despite higher
predictive value. This reveals an important distinction between two
dimensions of retrieval quality: \emph{citability} (measured by
faithfulness) and \emph{informativeness} (measured by macro F1). Graph
RAG achieves the better trade-off for prediction, while BM25 achieves
higher faithfulness at the cost of lower predictive accuracy.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The faithfulness--accuracy trade-off (RQ1).}
The results reveal a clear trade-off between accuracy and faithfulness.
LLM-only achieves the highest accuracy (0.618) because it can draw on
extensive parametric knowledge about how social emotions manifest in
television dialogue, a genre well-represented in LLM training corpora.
However, its 98.4\% unsupported claim rate indicates that this accuracy
is not grounded in the conversational evidence. Retrieval-augmented
methods constrain the model to reason from specific utterances,
slightly reducing accuracy but raising faithfulness by an order of
magnitude.

\paragraph{Graph RAG as the balanced choice (RQ2).}
To formalise the claim that Graph RAG provides the best balance between
prediction quality and faithfulness, the harmonic mean $H$ of normalised
macro F1 and faithfulness is computed for each method. Normalising macro
F1 to $[0,1]$ by dividing by the maximum observed value (0.491) and
faithfulness similarly (by 0.269), Graph RAG achieves $H = 0.67$,
exceeding BM25 RAG ($H = 0.60$), Text-only RAG ($H = 0.64$), and
Full-context LLM ($H = 0.11$). Graph RAG is the only method that is
not dominated by any other on either metric simultaneously: Full-context
LLM matches its macro F1 (0.491) but collapses to faithfulness 0.033;
BM25 RAG achieves the highest faithfulness (0.269) but lags in macro F1
(0.453). Retrieval does introduce a precision--recall trade-off: when
the retrieved context misses the most relevant emotional cue, the model
may be misled by irrelevant evidence, which partly explains why BM25 and
Dense RAG underperform text-only methods in macro F1.

\paragraph{Same-speaker history as the dominant signal (RQ3).}
The ablation study identifies same-speaker history as the most
informative graph component. This is consistent with psychological
findings that an individual's emotional trajectory is auto-correlated:
a speaker who was angry in a recent turn is more likely to remain
angry or transition to related states~\cite{hazarika2018conversational}.
Structural encoding of this continuity allows the graph to surface
more relevant evidence than unstructured flat retrieval.

\paragraph{Cross-dataset consistency of Graph RAG.}
The EmoryNLP results replicate the main MELD finding: Graph RAG achieves
the highest macro F1 (0.471) and accuracy (0.500), and the faithfulness
ordering across methods is consistent with MELD. The margin between
retrieval and non-retrieval methods is even larger on EmoryNLP (0.145
macro F1 gap between Graph RAG and LLM-only, versus 0.006 on MELD),
suggesting that graph-structured evidence selection is particularly
beneficial when the evaluation domain differs from the LLM's training
distribution. EmoryNLP's five-class distribution (joy 35.8\%, neutral
34.6\%, fear 11\%, anger 10\%, sadness 8.6\%) is moderately imbalanced
but does not prevent meaningful differentiation between methods, in
contrast to datasets with extreme single-class dominance.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations should be noted. First, both the inference model and
faithfulness judge are \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, which may introduce
self-evaluation bias; future work should validate the faithfulness metric
against human annotations on a held-out sample. Second, all methods use
the same evidence-constrained prompt, which may structurally disadvantage
methods with less citable evidence. Third, the graph construction relies
on heuristic edge detection (same-speaker links, rule-based emotion-shift
detection) that has not been independently validated. Fourth, experiments
are limited to 500 instances per dataset with a single random seed;
statistical significance of small macro F1 differences (e.g., 0.011
between Graph RAG and Text-only RAG on MELD) should be interpreted with
caution. Finally, the audio-feature ablation variant introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:ablation} reflects an edge-type heuristic rather than
actual audio processing; renaming this component in future work would
improve clarity.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a comparative evaluation of retrieval-augmented
and graph-augmented LLM methods for evidence-grounded emotion recognition
in social dialogue. A key contribution is the introduction of a
claim-level faithfulness evaluation framework that measures whether
model explanations are supported by retrieved evidence.

The experiments on MELD demonstrate that non-retrieval LLM methods,
despite achieving high accuracy, produce explanations that are almost
entirely unsupported by the conversational evidence (98.4\% unsupported
claims). Graph-augmented RAG achieves the best balance between prediction
quality and faithfulness among all evaluated methods — the only
approach that simultaneously achieves macro F1 above 0.49 and
faithfulness above 0.22 — with same-speaker history identified as the
most critical structural component. On EmoryNLP, Graph RAG again achieves the highest macro F1 (0.471) and
accuracy (0.500), and the faithfulness ordering across methods is
consistent with MELD. The cross-dataset consistency of both classification
and faithfulness findings strengthens confidence in the main conclusions.

These findings highlight faithfulness as a necessary complement to
accuracy for evaluating LLM-based ERC systems in socially sensitive
settings. Future work should investigate learned graph construction
strategies, label-balanced prompting for imbalanced datasets,
cross-dataset generalisation, and the relationship between faithfulness
and human-perceived explanation quality.
